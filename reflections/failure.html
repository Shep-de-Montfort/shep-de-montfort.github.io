Failure

June 2026

It took me 25 years to understand that I had a losing relationship with failure. Not too long ago, I would've thought avoiding failure meant the same thing as saying 'I always try to succeed', and in a discrete competition where there is a winner and a loser, these two thoughts do mean the same thing. But in life, the difference appears in the situations we quietly curate for ourselves, and that curation is actually just a ceiling on our own effort.

Growing up in the 2000s, kids and parents alike mocked the previous generation for handing out participation trophies, believing they falsely celebrated mediocrity and diminished actual winners. The belief was that competition dynamics existed in the real world, and it is better for kids to get exposed to them sooner rather than later.

I still think the 'everyone gets a trophy' attitude is not the right lesson, but I also think the 'only winners get trophies' attitude is not quite right either. The latter teaches that things are only worth doing if you win, and therefore if the chance of failure is likely, then what is the point of doing it in the first place?

The obvious answer is that you should compete in anything you love, regardless of outcome. And for me, this was High School Speech and Debate. I would show up to compete in every tournament even if it meant losing would mean public humiliation. I consider getting dominated in a debate somewhat similar to a fighter getting knocked out. Losing in fighting, as opposed to other sports, means your opponent is physically superior to you. Losing badly in debate is a display of someone beating up on your intelligence. You leave thinking that your opponent is just smarter than you. Our intelligence and our physicality are fuller representations of who we are than, say, swinging a bat. Regardless of the risk, I did it anyway because I loved debating. But maybe I wouldn't have done it if I wasn't also good at it. The competitions we choose are usually ones where our love for it and belief in our own ability are enough to offset the fear of losing.

Even in competitions we love, most of us still find ways to avoid the hardest tests. In a debate tournament, I wanted to face the worst people I could along the path to victory. In fighting, I often see the same mentality. Most top-contender fighters ask to fight the easiest opponents they can on the way to fighting for 'the title'. This act is so common it has its own term: 'ducking'. In fairness to these fighters though, they have good reason to 'duck'. Ducking can help them win the title while avoiding fights where they have a high probability of losing. Unsurprisingly though, the best of an already elite group of "world champions" are usually the ones who take the hard path of fighting the absolute best in the world to prove to themselves they are the best, even if that risks never being called a champion. Dana White, the matchmaker for the UFC, shared Islam Makhachev's response when being asked to take on an opponent on short notice: "What does this title represent? It means you're the best in the world. And if you're the best in the world, it doesn't matter who is standing across from you. What do I say? No? Never. Let's do this."

In an even more camouflaged way than fighting, our lives are structured around trophies that trick us into curating the easiest path to get them. Kindergarten through grade 12 can be summarized as one long competition for college admissions. The trophy comes in the form of a sweater you wear around school at the end of senior year. While sweater pageantry would be confusing to an alien, every kid and parent knows the rules: a Stanford sweater is better than a Nevada sweater. Fourteen years of a student's life summarized by letters printed on cotton. And just like the belt fighters wear, it did not matter how you got there. So you might as well duck your way into a sweater you would be proud to show off at the pageant. Here are a handful of ways I did it.

My freshman year, I took Spanish. The class required real effort to do well on the tests, homework, and essays. In Nevada, there were also a lot of native Spanish speakers taking the class; I saw this as a disadvantage for me. Spanish posed a threat to a critical aspect of my college competition scorecard: GPA. I decided to switch into the dead language across the hall: Latin. My friends told me that the Latin teacher never made them do any work, handed out A's, and you could still finish with a GPA boost by senior year by taking AP Latin. It was an easy decision.

Course selection wasn't the only place I ducked. I always copied homework from friends. It was the easiest way to get my time back while still getting credit; colleges weren't going to test me on what I retained in AP Environmental Science. I was content to cram before a test and forget everything the moment it was over.

Extracurriculars weren't immune to ducking either. The hack was finding things that looked impressive but required little effort. Student government was the king of the list. My Sophomore year I actually ran against a well-liked Junior which came with a high chance of failure. Since I was expected to lose, there was no embarrassment risk. Luckily, I won the popularity contest with a few memes. In my Junior year I had the option to run for president, but now I was the well-known person. The chance of failure was one I was no longer willing to take even though Student President was one of the only positions in Student Government with power. I elected to run uncontested for some other position that would round out my story to colleges.

After curating a record of competitions I knew I could win, I applied to college. Cornell thought my record was good enough to win their trophy, but even they probably thought my experience was too curated. They offered me the Transfer Option: spend a year at another school, hit a certain GPA, and attend Cornell sophomore year. Those poor suckers… did they not know college had the same reward mechanisms and was just as easy to duck?

I still needed somewhere to spend that first year, so I chose from the schools I had already applied to. I chose University of Miami because it would be the easiest school to earn the required GPA while being a fun place to mess around for a year. Even at an easy school like University of Miami, I curated my experience to only compete in things I knew I could win. The formula was simple: find the easier professor, take the easier class.

A year later, I had made it. I was finally at an Ivy League institution. But I had another choice to make. I could switch to a hard major, learn real skills from world class professors, and risk finishing with a blemished GPA among students smarter than me. Or I could stick to the major I knew I could win and protect my GPA.

At this point, I knew that choosing to learn hard skills such as programming in computer science would benefit me more than taking 'business arts' classes. But I was scared of failing; Cornell was full of students smarter than me who had grown up coding. I could also justify my cowardice easily enough. The goal of college was to win the trophy of having a prestigious first job. And luckily, careers in finance, law, and business paid just as well as engineering or medicine, but didn't require any of the hard skills or difficult classes to get there.

I chose a major I knew I would win at Cornell: Industrial and Labor Relations. Win I did. I never got less than an A, and I always felt at or near the top of my classes. I remember in Labor Economics, some of my classmates who were clearly more adept at literary arts struggled at multiplying fractions, and I enjoyed running circles around them. Likely in the building next door, other students were taking classes like discrete math while we were multiplying fractions. Both those struggling with the fractions and those taking differential equations were pushing themselves, I was not.

After a semester or two of straight A's in winnable classes, I was feeling like a big fish in a small pond. But I had this unshakeable feeling that something was wrong. It is hard to diagnose even now, but perhaps it was seeing my engineering friends learning useful skills like MATLAB, or perhaps I finally just had a strong desire to test myself. I decided to enroll in econometrics.

When I signed up for econometrics, I was warned I probably should not do it because statistics and advanced probability modeling were prerequisites. I had only taken a three-week statistics course over Christmas break and never took advanced probability modeling. The other students weren't ILR students; they were stats majors, engineers, and computer scientists. When I told the professor I was taking this class with just three weeks of stats, he said 'what are you, crazy?' He was more shocked than discouraging, and turned out to be a great and kind professor. I had never signed up for something where the chance of failure was so high. But I was determined to do it.

For the first couple of weeks, I had no idea what was going on because the material built on prerequisites I never took. I was so far behind classmates for whom the material came naturally. But the most useful thing I had observed at Cornell up to that point was that while people were intellectually smart, they either didn't work extremely hard or didn't take advantage of useful tactics to accelerate their learning. At an Ivy League, I thought the front of the class would be fought over. I thought office hours would be packed. Neither were true. Students skipped class and office hours were usually empty. I committed to doing whatever it took to learn econometrics. I studied on my own for hours and spent countless hours in my professor's office hours. I ended with an A. I learned more in econometrics than in any other class, and it was my first taste that the risk of failure is a barometer for how much room you have to grow and learn.

But even after econometrics, I played it safe more often than not to ensure my credentials were unblemished when applying to good jobs. Once again my cowardice was rewarded by earning a prestigious consulting job, and the incentives to duck did not stop here either. Even in our careers, ducking can be the easiest way to get a promotion or keep your job. This is especially true in consulting, where you are measured across many discrete projects with different teams. So just like high school and college, you can seek out easy projects with lenient managers to avoid the risk of failure. This is exactly what I did when I started.

After a streak of home run projects, I felt the same urge I had when choosing to take econometrics: I wanted to do something difficult. I voluntarily signed up for a tough four-week project over winter break instead of cruising into the new year. Unlike econometrics, this did not go well. I got bad feedback, and my reputation from two years of strong work was now tarnished by the result of this project. However, I learned more in those four weeks than in any comparable stretch of easy work because I was doing everything I could not to fail. And every project after has been easy in comparison.

So what has changed? Why did I finally stop taking the easy route? By now, I had won most of the superficial trophies I was competing for. I went to a good college, got a good first job, and stayed long enough for people to think I was good at that job. But I have encountered a few problems: I don't know what I want to do, and I have not developed a core set of skills I am confident in.

Avoiding failure has been an attempt to control my environment instead of control who I am. I have ended up here because that is where I let the tailwinds blow me. As I've gotten older and realized how fast time moves, I want to be more intentional about where I spend it. Putting myself in uncomfortable situations earlier would have given me a better foundation for knowing where I want to go and the confidence to pursue it.

I know I want to spend my time creating something: maybe a product, an idea, a company, or a movement. Creation is building something from nothing, and I cannot bring my past trophies into that process. All I can bring is myself and the skills I have built.

What I can create is confined within the circle of knowledge and experience I possess. Failure avoidance has shrunk that circle by keeping me from the very experiences that would have grown it. A hole is actually a better image than a circle, because knowledge builds on itself. It is easy to avoid failure by digging a wide shallow hole across many topics. But committing deeply to one thing and failing at it is more personal, it feels like a verdict on who you are. Regardless, the right approach is always working to expand that circle, even if it means failing and taking some setbacks along the way.

The skills I passed on out of fear of failure have real consequences. I cannot build relationships or opportunities in Spanish. I do not have the engineering skills to create physical products or the math skills to build financial algorithms. The things I have created, Recess, a social software company, and Nightcrawl, a workout events company, taught me that the skills and experiences in my toolkit mattered far more than my GPA, the college I went to, or the size of my bonus.

To tie everything together, our early life teaches us there are discrete winners and losers, which conditions us to only enter situations we can win. But here is the fallacy: while there can only be one high score on a test, everyone can still get better at the subject by putting themselves out there. Just because you won't finish at the top doesn't mean you shouldn't compete. Over time, and especially in creation, there is no such thing as winners and losers. The only thing that matters is how much you have grown your circle. I now live in Miami where the connections and opportunities I could have built by learning Spanish, even broken Spanish, would be worth far more than the A I got in a language nobody speaks. The value you can create is only restricted by yourself.

So at 25, for the first time, I am deliberately choosing the harder path. I am learning programming from scratch, understanding how AI actually works rather than just using the tools that abstract it away. This means sitting with confusion, moving slowly, and being a beginner again. But that discomfort is exactly the point.
