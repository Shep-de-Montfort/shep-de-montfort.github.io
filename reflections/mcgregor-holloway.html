McGregor vs Holloway II

July 2026

This weekend Conor McGregor and Max Holloway headlined International Fight Week at UFC 329. This fight card was marketed as "the return of McGregor", the sport's biggest star, who had not fought in 5 years after breaking his leg versus Dustin Poirier in their trilogy fight. Ten years prior to UFC 329, McGregor was on a historic run, becoming the first world champion simultaneously in two divisions and knocking out legendary fighters along the way.

Between McGregor's double-champ status 10 years ago and his leg break 5 years ago, he stepped away from MMA for a boxing match with Floyd Mayweather. Despite losing, the bout was a huge financial success, boosting McGregor's wealth and notoriety. Once McGregor returned to MMA after his 2-year boxing layoff, many say that his boxing training had a negative influence on his previously successful MMA style. Others argue McGregor's newfound wealth and stratospheric fame took him away from his dedicated training lifestyle. McGregor was involved in launching a whiskey brand, a menswear fashion line, and his own promotions company during his time away from MMA. When Conor came back, he faced his antithesis: Khabib Nurmagomedov. Khabib focused on nothing but MMA-relevant skills since he was eight years old. Khabib was not riding his undefeated record for fame or business; he maintained his focus only on being a UFC champion. Khabib inevitably 'smashed' McGregor, submitting him in the fourth round.

McGregor's loss to Khabib was a huge stain on his larger-than-life persona of invincibility. However, even after losing to Khabib and up until his leg break fight with Poirier, McGregor still had a fight and a goal in mind. He still had ambition pulling him forward. After the leg break, that ambition was gone. McGregor would be seen partying, drinking, doing drugs, among other out-of-the-cage antics while living large off the money he had made. Most importantly, McGregor never set foot again in the octagon for a competitive bout in those 5 years.

The last bit of context needed to understand UFC 329 is that Max Holloway had fought 8 times between McGregor breaking his leg and their rematch. These were 8 high-level fights against the best in the promotion; four of the matches were against fighters who have been UFC champions. For another point of reference, the average UFC fighter has roughly 1.7 bouts per year, and the average UFC career is less than 4 fights, according to recent data.

With all that backstory, all you need to know about UFC 329 is that McGregor suffered a knee injury in the first minute of the first round, ending the fight with a Max Holloway victory. The more interesting thing about UFC 329, to me, was people's hopes for McGregor going in. Anecdotally, many people on the internet were hopeful for a vintage McGregor victory. All of my friends were hoping for a McGregor win. Notable UFC fighters like Tom Aspinall, Sean O'Malley, Dricus du Plessis, and Merab Dvalishvili all either picked McGregor to win or expressed that they were deeply pulling for him. On Polymarket, McGregor was trading at a 30% chance to win.

The people rooting for McGregor had the same reasons as those placing money on him. They believed the skills he displayed on his golden run were still present. They believed in McGregor's redemption arc. However, this redemption story is different from one where someone loses a match, trains hard, then comes back and wins. McGregor's redemption arc is one where he avenges himself — not a loss to an opponent, but a loss to his own circumstances and poor decision-making. The idea was that one year of super-focused training was enough to offset four years of inactivity and undisciplined living.

Stories like McGregor's are ones that we often romanticize. However, if you stop and think about it, this is not the story we should desire, and we should be thankful it rarely plays out this way.

Let's say that UFC 329 had a different ending: Conor McGregor looked even better than he did 10 years ago and knocked out Max Holloway inside one round in dominating fashion. What would this alternative story portray? It would portray that it does not matter that McGregor was not fighting for five years, and it does not matter that he spent those years drinking and partying. It would mean that McGregor's natural ability both overcomes his own destructive habits and beats Holloway's 5 years of consistent, hard training. If these are the stories we want to believe in, then we are cheering for only those born with exceptional talents to succeed. We are cheering for the invalidation of people's hard work. Rooting for stories like McGregor's is rooting for hard work to not pay off, which means rooting for a world where hard work never reliably works for anyone, including yourself. Would you not want to be confident that five years of consistent, disciplined work yields a better result than a year of hasty, over-compensated training? In the very best interpretation, one would be cheering for McGregor to get lucky, which still means Holloway's 5 years of hard work meant nothing.

In a way, UFC 329 has a pretty happy ending. Max Holloway's hard work was rewarded — another reminder that hard work pays off. We should be thankful that what you put in tends to reflect what you get out. If we had too many examples where this was not true, there would be literally no reason to try. At the same time, the result can also be a positive for McGregor; he too can learn from this principle. His number of fights in the past 5 years went from zero to one, regardless of the outcome. And, even though it is unlikely he will ever be a world champion again, the path of consistent work will keep him healthier and happier than when he was not working towards anything.
