Nightcrawl

Pioneering “nightlife fitness”

One day, I was flying home to Miami from a work trip in New Jersey, and I heard a really awesome EDM song. I thought to myself that this would be incredible to work out to. My next thought was that it would be even better to work out to a live DJ playing during a workout. I paused and realized I had never seen a DJ play while people worked out. Miami felt like the perfect place to start this because it obviously has the nightlife culture, but it also has a massive counterculture of people who do not want to drink and party, and would rather do something healthier. The problem was that the party scene completely dominated weekends, which led to a bad case of FOMO for the health-conscious crowd. I pitched the idea of a Friday night warehouse workout hosted with a real live DJ to Grant, and he was immediately in.

The first thing we needed to figure out was where on Earth we were going to host this thing. We found an awesome old airplane hangar owned by a local production studio. We told them what we were planning, and luckily, they were down. From there, we needed to lock down liability insurance, a working ticketing platform, workout equipment, sound and lighting gear, a DJ, and obviously, guests.

When you added everything up, the upfront costs were looking horrendous, but I had learned from my first startup, Recess, how to move fast and execute on a budget. I scoured the market to find the cheapest insurance that still protected us from injury liability and met the hangar property damage requirements. Next up was sound and lighting, and this was a massive pain. The process taught me how to efficiently source and negotiate with vendors. Just like in consulting, I knew absolutely nothing about audio engineering or lighting setups at the start. But by the end of sourcing vendors for our first event, I knew exactly what our setup should look like for eighty people in a seven thousand square foot enclosed indoor space with a difficult acoustic profile, and exactly what it should cost. Working with the venue and the production vendors gave me great lessons in negotiating price effectively while still building strong, lasting relationships.

The traditional equipment needed for the workout we planned would have cost upwards of $20,000, so we found scrappy ways to manufacture it ourselves. We built heavy weights for farmers carries by filling water jugs with sand. We made heavy sandbags to replace dumbbells by buying $20 military duffel bags and packing them with mulch, sand, and gravel bags from Home Depot. Instead of buying a fitness tire for $1,000, we went down to the shipyard and the workers gave us a massive one for free. The most difficult part of the logistics was pulling all of this off without owning a car.

For ticketing, we reached out to Sweatpals to ask how to best configure our page to drive conversions. They were fantastic and even helped cross-promote the event to their community.

The last piece of the puzzle was actually getting people through the door. One thing I took away from building ventures in the past is that when you are first starting out, you have to do things that do not scale and get all your friends to show up. So, I pestered everyone I knew in Miami to come. Grant did the same. Simultaneously, we printed out little business cards, went to other local wellness events to genuinely embed ourselves in the community, and pitched every single person we talked to. Lastly, we ran a serious outbound campaign on Instagram. Operating from an account with only a hundred followers, we reached out to hundreds of Miami influencers, presenting our brand with the scale, polished aesthetic, and professionalism of a company ten times our size to get them on the guest list. We offered free tickets for them and their friends. Out of hundreds of messages, our hit rate was only about ten to fifteen influencers who actually showed up, but that was all the spark we needed.

The day of our first event arrived, and it was a smash hit. Everyone loved the music, the warehouse venue, the crowd energy, and the aesthetic of everyone wearing all black. Off the back of the influencer content and event momentum, we gained thousands of followers overnight, and our second event sold out weeks in advance. For some of our later events, we were lucky enough to secure partnerships with major national nutrition brands and local hospitality groups. We had people across the country asking when we were bringing the concept to their city, and some even flew into Miami just to attend.

We capped off a massive run and ultimately stopped hosting events in early February 2026.
