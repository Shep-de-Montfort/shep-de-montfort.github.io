2022–2025 Entrepreneurship

Recess

My first startup

One's first startup is a lot like one's first love. The idea of bringing videogame-style matchmaking to in-person sports first hit me in winter 2020 during the pandemic, when my family was in Texas and I knew absolutely nobody. I loved the concept of launching my own software startup, but as a non-technical founder, I had no clue how to actually build it. When I got to Cornell, I reached out to a few students on app development project teams to ask how to find a co-founder. After demonstrating that I literally did not know the difference between Figma and Ligma, they rightly laughed in my face. They explained that everyone has an idea, and I was no different, plus I had no compensation to offer them. They pointed out that the bar on campus is incredibly high because the talented engineers are already heavily recruited and highly paid by FAANG companies, so they had no reason to take a risk on a random non-technical guy. I realized I had a massive amount to learn if I was going to pull this off.

A year later, my break finally came during a February break dinner when 95% of campus had left for the holidays, and I met Harrison. He was on Cornell's premier app development team, Cornell App Dev, had an upcoming gig at Google Maps, and deeply loved sports. I gave him a highly refined pitch, and he was in. Harrison educated me on the reality of what we needed, which was a full squad of designers, frontend engineers, and backend engineers. We immediately went to work. We pitched Recess at Cornell's Half-Baked startup event, only to find the audience was entirely composed of other founders waiting to pitch their own ideas. Realizing good things never come easily, we bypassed traditional routes and manually recruited a scrawny initial team using Harrison's App Dev connections, targeted emails, and cold outreach.

Managing a team of students with zero funding meant everyone was working strictly for equity, which brought a mountain of operational challenges. I had to draft the equity agreements, make the tough calls to cut underperforming team members, and keep everyone motivated to spend their precious non-academic hours coding. I made plenty of classic founder mistakes, like failing to test user demand early on and refusing to scope down the MVP. Instead, we spent two years building a massive, hyper-complex product that included skill-based matchmaking, real-time court availability indexing, check-in verification, winner tracking, sportsmanship scores, equipment coordination, social chat, profile pages with Apex Legends-style badges, and even planned tournament architectures.

Despite the bloated scope and high attrition, I kept the team building over summers, through graduations, and eventually into my own full-time career at BCG. I would pull consulting work until 2 AM, hop on DevOps calls with Gonzalo until 5 AM, and still make my 8 AM BCG meetings, dedicating every weekend to the company. I learned to lead by example, covering every single business charge out of my own pocket and even having the engineers bet on my CS 1110 exam scores so they knew I was actively putting in the work to understand their world. I learned how to be a great judge of talent and create a great working culture. When our original designers left, I brought on my best friend, Jay. Even though he was just getting into design and did not have much initial experience, I realized I could spend 500 hours on a call coordinating and working far better with a trusted friend than with a random hire who might have been better on hour one but nowhere near as elite by hour 500. When our backend slowed down, I had Gonzalo pull in Su Yean from my intro CS class because I knew he was reliable, even if he lacked experience. By the end, we had forged a core, fiercely loyal team that stayed for a year and a half and never quit until I officially called the shutdown.

When the app finally launched, the engineering team was uncomfortable with marketing, so I took vacation days from BCG and flew back to Ithaca to hit the pavement. I set a strict goal of 100 downloads, walking up to every single person I saw on campus and pitching Recess face-to-face. It taught me that marketing is a pure numbers game, where grinding through nine rejections to get one download in an hour is identical to talking to one receptive person for an hour.

Ultimately, the lack of early validation killed us. We never achieved more than a few active matches on campus because early adopters were completely slaughtered by the network effect. Someone would log on looking for a ping-pong match, find an empty queue, and delete the app. Because we spent two years building an all-in-one MVP rather than launching fast, we were too exhausted and tied down by full-time jobs to execute a successful pivot. You can watch every Y Combinator video on earth, but you will always find a reason why their warnings don't apply to your specific vision. Going through the actual pain of these mistakes gave me genuine founder instincts and made me a profoundly better entrepreneur, and I am incredibly proud that we pushed through the drama to build a highly complex product that actually worked.
